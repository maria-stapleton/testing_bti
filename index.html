1800s

US & UK | Shifting Gender Norms

1820s men’s dress — Fashion Museum Bath

As the 1800s progressed men in the west started steering away from what is now recognised as ‘feminising markers’ and morally illegitimate — this included in dress where colours, pattern and frills; cosmetics stopped being used; and moving into the 1900s fragrance became gendered. This shift impacted other markets, notably Japan which began adopting more gender distinctions through appearance.

1900s

US & UK | Body Hair Removal

Gillette ad, 1917

It was during the First World War that Gillette released a razor in the United States for Women to remove the hair under their arms. In the 1920s, as hemlines raised products for hair removal also started being advertised and used.

Beauty Imagined: A History of the Global Beauty Industry, Chapter 3

US | Annie Turnbo Malone Brand Poro Soars

Poro Preparations Pamphlet from Poro College Company, 1920-27

Annie Malone, born in 1869 to formerly enslaved parents develops a hair and cosmetics brand and beauty school that is incredibly successful. Malone recognises three things: appearance to African American women is intrinsically related to the discrimination they face; their is a gap in the market for hair straightening (to meet American beauty standards) while keeping hair and scalp healthy; and third, a demand for inclusive cosmetics. Malone became a successful and wealthy businesswoman, providing employment to other African American women and a keen philanthropist. The brand however did suffer after a divorce settlement and because of the Great Depression.

1950s

CHINA | Beauty Under Mao

1949 saw the end of the Chinese Civil War and the beginning of Mao’s leadership, lasting until 1976. During this period, beauty was defined as women who displayed darker skin, strength, resilience, and honest labour: this standard remained until the 70s.This was in sharp contrast to traditional Chinese beauty norms and wiped out previous western influences.

INDONESIA | Beauty in the Old Order

Purol advertisment featuring switch to Indonesian models

Sukarno’s presidency (1945-67) on the one hand reduced western influenced with some ads switching to Indonesian models, however, western beauty ideals remained sticky with Caucasian features still featuring regularly. This reflects the push and pull that many markets faced post independence between their national beauty and identity and western beauty standards.

INDIA | The Launch of Lakmé

After declaring independence India’s prime minister, Jawaharlal Nehru had the company Tata create an Indian owned cosmetics brand in 1952, Lakmé. The brand signals independence, but not without its complexities. It’s named after a French opera on the topic of British imperialism— occupying an ambiguous space.

US & UK | Hollywood Glamour

Marilyn Monroe, 1953. Gelatin silver print — Collection of Leon and Michaela Constantiner, Brooklyn Museum

In the post war period Hollywood icons such as Marilyn Monroe and Brigitte Bardot embodied the beauty standard. In contrast the the masculine silhouette seen during the Second World War the 50s embraced glamour and femininity: hair was voluminous and usually worn wavy, lips pouty and red, and an hourglass, voluptuous, figure the ideal.

US | Civil Rights Movement, Black is Beautiful

Photo shoot at a school for one of the many modeling groups who had begun to embrace natural hairstyles in the 1960s, 1966 — Kwame Brathwaite — Philip Martin Gallery, Los Angeles

The Civil Rights Movement (1954-1968) impacted beauty in several ways; with African Americans celebrating their identity as typified by the James Brown’s song Say It Loud—I’m Black and I’m Proud” (1968) the ‘Black and Beautiful’ movement emerged in parallel. This saw natural hair being embraced as a statement of resistance and pride as well as several new entrepreneurs entering the market. For instance, John H. Johnson, founder of the Ebony periodical launched Fashion Fair Cosmetics to address lack of shade ranges in base cosmetics.

US, UK & BRAZIL | Heightened Masculinity

London, 1951 — V & A

As many markets saw a standard of über femininity for women, men experienced a similar standard for masculinity. This was also seen in the marketing strategies of some brands, for instance Gillette used endorsements of figures in areas deemed ‘very masculine’ in the respected markets. Football in Brazil, boxing in the United States. And while actors such as Marlon Brando did embody masculinity and influenced men’s body standards to an extent, increasing pressures for a muscular body, this was felt more strongly amongst younger men.

INDIA | Success in Beauty Pageants

Sushmita Sen, 1994

In the 90s India saw great success at international beauty pageants: in 1992 made Sapre nearly won Miss Universe. The following year Namrata Shirodkhar was runner up in the same contest, and in 1994 Sushmita Sen grabbed the title. That same year Aishwarya Rai won Miss World and Sheetal Mallar was crowned international model of the year, other wins followed. India was claimed as the ‘new bona fide beauty superpower’. The success of the women upon there return, often achieving success in acting for instance and being subjects of great pride cemented beauty standards and contributed to increased beauty pressures. It wasn’t without controversy however, in 1996, when the Miss World pageant was held in South Indian city, Bangalore protests against the contest were held by a range of groups: religious leaders, women’s groups (both conservative and feminists), and political parties.

CHINA | First Artificial Pageant (2004)

Yang Yuan, 2004

The Pageant for Artificial Beauty emerged in 2004 after model Yang Yuan was disqualified from a contest for having undergone a procedure, on that topic, the first winner, Feng Qian states: “natural beauty and cosmetic surgery beauty are the same and they should be recognised as that”. This pageant is representative both of the tensions between natural beauty and plastic surgery present in most markets, and equally the growth that plastic surgery saw in China through the 1990s and 2000s, even if for many it remained controversial. Yang Yuan’s procedures point to the beauty pressures that exist, she made her face smaller and thinner, and losing weight.

GLOBAL | Dove Campaign for Real Beauty

Dove campaign, 2004

After conducting research and finding that tonly 2% of women would describe themselves as beautiful Dove launched the campaign for Real Beauty. It featured a more diverse cast of women in age, ethnicity, body size, and other characteristics. It was a monumental campaign that signalled a shift in marketing strategies — especially for mass brands.
